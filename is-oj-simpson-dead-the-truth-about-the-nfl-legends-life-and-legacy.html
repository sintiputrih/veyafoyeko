OJ Simpson’s Football Career

OJ Simpson was born on July 9, 1947, in San Francisco, California. He grew up in a poor and violent neighborhood, where he joined a street gang called the Persian Warriors. He won the Heisman Trophy in 1968, the most prestigious award in college football, and was a two-time unanimous All-American. He also helped USC win the national championship in 1967.

Simpson was drafted first overall by the Buffalo Bills in the 1969 NFL/AFL draft. He played for the Bills for nine seasons, from 1969 to 1977, and then for the San Francisco 49ers for two seasons, from 1978 to 1979. He was one of the most dominant and popular running backs in NFL history. He was a five-time Pro Bowler, a five-time first-team All-Pro, and the NFL MVP in 1973. He also led the league in rushing yards four times, in rushing touchdowns twice, and in points scored in 1975. He is widely regarded as one of the greatest running backs of all time.

OJ Simpson’s Murder Trial

Simpson’s life changed dramatically in 1994, when he was accused of murdering his ex-wife Nicole Brown Simpson and her friend Ronald Goldman. The murders took place on June 12, 1994, outside Brown’s condo in Brentwood, Los Angeles. Simpson was arrested on June 17, 1994, after a dramatic low-speed chase in his white Ford Bronco, which was broadcast live on national TV. He pleaded not guilty to the charges and hired a team of high-profile lawyers, known as the “Dream Team”, which included Johnnie Cochran, Robert Shapiro, F. Lee Bailey, Alan Dershowitz, and Robert Kardashian. The trial began on January 24, 1995, and lasted for nine months. It was one of the most notorious and publicized criminal trials in American history, dubbed as the “Trial of the Century”. It was also televised live on several networks, attracting millions of viewers.

The prosecution, led by Marcia Clark and Christopher Darden, presented a wealth of physical and circumstantial evidence against Simpson, such as DNA, blood, fingerprints, shoe prints, hair, fibers, and a bloody glove found at the crime scene and at Simpson’s estate. They also portrayed Simpson as a jealous and abusive husband, who had a history of domestic violence against Brown. The defense, led by Cochran, challenged the credibility and integrity of the evidence and the investigators, alleging that they were contaminated, planted, or mishandled. They also argued that Simpson was a victim of racial discrimination and police misconduct, especially by Detective Mark Fuhrman, who was accused of being a racist and a perjurer. They also introduced the famous phrase “If it doesn’t fit, you must acquit”, referring to Simpson’s inability to wear the bloody glove during a courtroom demonstration.

The jury, composed of nine African Americans, two whites, and one Hispanic, deliberated for less than four hours and reached a verdict on October 3, 1995. They found Simpson not guilty of both murders, to the shock and dismay of many people, especially the victims’ families. The verdict also sparked a heated debate and a deep division in the country along racial lines, as many African Americans celebrated Simpson’s acquittal, while many whites condemned it. The trial exposed the issues of race, class, gender, and media in the American justice system and society.
